Section 1

The Daily CEO Review Cadence

You do not need to babysit your agents. You need to know, in 15-20 minutes every morning, whether the machine is running or it isn't. Here is exactly what that looks like.

What to Check Every Morning

Check these (they matter):

  • Agent outputs that touch money, brand, or customers
  • Any flagged escalations from the previous 24 hours
  • Tasks that were due yesterday and show no completion
  • Any cross-department dependency blocking a P0 or P1

Skip these (they don't need you):

  • Routine content drafts that haven't been reviewed by your CMO agent yet
  • Internal status updates between agents on P2/P3 tasks
  • First drafts of anything, operational reports without anomalies, agent activity logs with no flags

Time Budget

BlockTimeWhat You're Doing
Escalation scan3 minRead flagged items from overnight
Priority task status5 minP0 and P1 items only
Decision queue5 minAnything waiting on you specifically
Quick replies5 minOne-line directives, approvals, rejections
Total~18 minDone. Close it.

What "Good Enough" Looks Like vs What Needs You

Good enough — let it go:

  • Task is moving. Owner is active. Deadline isn't in danger.
  • Draft output is 80% there and going through a review cycle.
  • Agent made a minor judgment call you'd have made differently but it doesn't change the outcome.

Needs your attention:

  • Deadline is today and there's no output yet.
  • An agent made a decision involving money, going public, or changing direction.
  • Two agents are blocked on each other and neither escalated it.
  • Something looks done but you haven't actually seen the output.

Adrian's Daily Checklist

DAILY CEO REVIEW — [DATE] [ ] Escalations from last 24 hours: ___ [ ] P0 tasks — status: ___ [ ] P1 tasks — status: ___ [ ] Decisions waiting on me: ___ [ ] Anything due today with no output: ___ [ ] One thing I need to unblock right now: ___ Total time: ___ min
Print it. Fill it by hand. The physical act of writing forces you to actually think about each line instead of skimming.
Section 2

The Trust/Override Framework

Every agent you work with earns a tier. Here's the system I use.

The 3-Tier System

Tier 1
Full Trust

The agent acts. You review outcomes, not decisions.

Tier 2
Verify Before Acting

The agent prepares. You approve before anything goes live or gets spent.

Tier 3
Always Escalate

The agent flags and presents options. You decide. Every time.

Which Tasks Go Where

Task TypeTierWhy
Internal drafts, research, analysisFull TrustLow consequence if wrong
Customer-facing contentVerifyBrand risk if off
Outbound campaignsVerifyReputation on the line
Spending decisionsAlways EscalateMoney leaving the business
Strategy pivotsAlways EscalateDirection changes are yours
Anything publicVerify or EscalateNo take-backs
Hiring, contracts, partnershipsAlways EscalateLegal and financial exposure

How to Level Agents Up from Verify to Full Trust

Promotion is earned, not given. An agent moves up when:

  1. It has completed 10+ tasks in the verify tier with zero corrections needed.
  2. Its output quality is consistent, not just occasionally good.
  3. It has handled at least one unexpected situation well without defaulting to "I don't know."
  4. You have reviewed enough of its work to predict how it handles edge cases.
Start narrow. Give it Full Trust on one task type first. Watch for 30 days. Then expand.

Red Flags That Always Trigger an Override

Override the agent immediately when:

  • It acts without being asked to act
  • It makes a spending decision it wasn't authorized to make
  • It produces output that contradicts something you've already decided
  • It goes public with anything without your explicit approval
  • It changes the scope of a task without flagging the change
  • Its output is inconsistent with previous work and offers no explanation
One override doesn't mean the agent is broken. A pattern of the same override means it's in the wrong tier.
Section 3

The 3 Decisions You Never Delegate to an Agent

Decision 1: Real Money Leaving the Business Above Pre-Set Thresholds

An agent optimizing for results will find ways to spend. It doesn't feel the weight of cash outflow the way you do. It doesn't know your current runway, your upcoming obligations, or what a $3,000 mistake means this month specifically. It only knows the task.

What can go wrong: Ad spend escalates without a human check. Vendor payments get authorized twice. Software subscriptions stack up because each one looked justified in isolation.

The fix: Set hard dollar thresholds. Below the threshold, agents operate. Above it, they flag and wait. No exceptions.

Decision 2: Anything Public-Facing That Could Damage the Brand

Agents are fast. Fast is the problem here. A wrong angle on a tweet, a customer complaint handled poorly in public, a launch email that goes out before you've seen it — these aren't recoverable with a follow-up. First impressions write themselves in permanent ink.

What can go wrong: An agent drafts a response to a public controversy using the wrong tone. It goes out before you review it. Screenshots live forever.

The fix: Nothing customer-facing or media-facing goes live without a final human pass. Build this into the workflow, not the exception handling.

Decision 3: Any Strategic Pivot

Speed adjustments are fine. Doing the same thing faster, or pausing a campaign to optimize it — those are execution calls. Changing direction entirely is a CEO call. The agents don't have the full context of where you're going. They know the task, not the three-year picture.

What can go wrong: Your CMO agent sees poor campaign performance and pivots the messaging to something that performs better short-term but contradicts your positioning. You realize six months later you've drifted off-brand.

The fix: Define what counts as a pivot versus an optimization. Agents optimize. You pivot.

Section 4

The Weekly Org Health Check

Every Monday. 15 minutes. No exceptions.

5 Questions to Ask Yourself Each Week

  1. Is every P0 and P1 task either complete or actively moving with a clear owner?
  2. Has every department head given me at least one meaningful output this week, not just status updates?
  3. Is anything that was Green last week now Yellow or Red, and do I know why?
  4. Am I the bottleneck anywhere right now?
  5. If I disappeared for 48 hours, what would break first?

How to Spot Agent Drift Before It Costs You

Drift is slow. That's what makes it dangerous. Watch for:

  • Outputs getting shorter or less specific without explanation
  • An agent that used to escalate is now making calls it shouldn't
  • Quality that was excellent two months ago that now requires significant edits
  • An agent solving the wrong version of the problem
If you spot drift, don't wait. Recalibrate now. Drift compounds.

What to Do When an Agent Is Underperforming

  1. Pull the last 5 outputs. Find the pattern.
  2. Identify whether it's a brief quality problem (you) or an execution problem (the agent).
  3. If it's the brief: rewrite your templates.
  4. If it's the agent: drop it one tier in the trust framework and re-run recent work with tighter parameters.
  5. If it doesn't improve in 2 weeks: replace or restructure the role.

Weekly Org Health Check Format

WEEKLY ORG HEALTH — [DATE] ATLAS (CTO): [ ] Green [ ] Yellow [ ] Red Notes: ___ VALENCIA (CMO): [ ] Green [ ] Yellow [ ] Red Notes: ___ STERLING (CFO): [ ] Green [ ] Yellow [ ] Red Notes: ___ SENTINEL (COO): [ ] Green [ ] Yellow [ ] Red Notes: ___ NOVA (CPO): [ ] Green [ ] Yellow [ ] Red Notes: ___ Top bottleneck this week: ___ One thing I'm fixing by Friday: ___
Yellow means watch it. Red means act today.
Section 5

Adrian's Department Briefing Template

Skip a field and you will spend twice as long fixing the output. Fill it out completely and the agent does exactly what you needed.

The Template

DEPARTMENT BRIEFING Task: [One sentence. What are you asking for?] Priority: [P0 / P1 / P2 / P3] Owner: [Which agent or department is responsible?] Deadline: [Specific date and time. Not "ASAP."] Success Criteria: [How will you know this is done correctly?] Output Format: [Document / Email draft / Spreadsheet / Verbal summary / etc.] Context: [What does the agent need to know that isn't obvious?] Constraints: [What should it NOT do? Any limits on scope, tone, budget?]

Example Filled-In Briefing

DEPARTMENT BRIEFING Task: Write a 5-email welcome sequence for new CallFluent AI trial users. Priority: P1 Owner: VALENCIA (CMO) via QUILL Deadline: Friday, March 21 by 5PM EET Success Criteria: Five complete email drafts, each under 300 words, with subject line, preview text, and body. Emails build on each other and drive toward booking a setup call by email 3. Output Format: Google Doc with one email per section. Context: Trial users have already signed up. They haven't completed setup. Most churn in the first 7 days. Needs to feel personal, not automated. Constraints: No em-dashes. No "just checking in" language. No discounts in emails 1-3. Adrian's voice throughout.
